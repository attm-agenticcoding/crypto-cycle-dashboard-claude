Convex dial vs causal posterior · which deploys better, using only data available at the time?

Both policies are causal — each decides week t from data available at t only; the realized low is ground truth used for grading, never by a policy. The convex dial (the live headline; γ=2.0, deep-anchor ×0.6) has shape constants that were tuned on these same bears, so this table is biased toward it. The causal posterior sizes to the bottom-price posterior mass price has already fallen through and has no tunable deployment shape.
Cost basis is only half the ledger. A cheap average entry is worthless if the policy barely participates, so the table also grades mission risk: missed rec = the recovery gain the never-deployed cash gave up vs simply buying the low; regret if low-in = the terminal shortfall vs going all-in at the realized low — i.e. the cost if the low has already happened and no cheaper price ever comes. On the real bears the posterior deploys nearly fully with lower regret than the dial; its under-participation cost shows up in the non-bear stress windows (the 2019 dip, the COVID fast-V), where holding back is arguably correct for a cycle-bottom mandate — the columns let you judge that trade rather than hide it.
Evidence vs policy. The posterior’s primitive output is a probability (P(further low)); the deploy % shown is that probability run through one explicit, trivial mapping — deploy in proportion to the downside mass already passed (1−CDF). That mapping is a convention, not a proven-optimal ‘correct buy amount’; this table measures the realized deployment quality of that convention. The allocation decision (reserves, throttle, fallback) stays the deployment policy’s job.
“Parameter-free” means no fitted deployment shape — it is NOT assumption-free. The posterior still assumes: (a) the bottom sits near one of four structural floors (MVRV×realized-price, Mayer/200-week-MA, age power-law, per-cycle drawdown-decay — floors.py); (b) each floor’s uncertainty is lognormal with a dispersion measured from past lows, never below a fixed σ-floor; (c) the floors combine by inverse-variance weight (ensemble.py); (d) human-labelled prior cycle-low dates + documented pre-2014 drawdown constants; (e) regularisation constants (σ-floors, drift cap, clip ranges). What it avoids is fitting a deployment curve to the cycles it is judged on. 2026 is incomplete — its ‘low’ is just today’s price, so premium / terminal / mission-risk are not yet outcomes.

Decision layer · research: turning the probability into an allocation (expected-regret sizing) — not the live headline

This is research evidence, not the live recommendation — the headline is unchanged. The pure posterior gives a probability; the expected-regret policy is the missing decision layer — it chooses the deploy fraction that minimizes expected regret under the posterior, weighing buying-too-early against missing the recovery if the low is already in, and shrinking toward caution where an asset’s history is thin. On the four real bears it deploys cheaper and ends higher than either the tuned dial or the raw 1−CDF, and it cures the posterior’s fast-V under-participation without a calendar/time-floor (the participation comes from the missed-recovery term in the loss). Its loss is parameter-light, not parameter-free: a declared recovery scale ρ=1.0, confidence σ-inflation, and a draw clip — all fixed before the results (DECISION_LAYER.md) and shown to be perturbation-robust. Adoption is owner-gated and per-asset: by the confidence gate BTC is eligible; ETH stays on the dial until its on-chain history thickens.
